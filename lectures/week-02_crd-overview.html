Introduction

In mathematical statistics, well-structured experimental designs and robust analytical methods are essential for drawing valid conclusions about treatment effects. One of the simplest yet fundamental designs is the completely randomized design (CRD). Under a CRD, each experimental unit is randomly assigned to one of several treatment conditions without any blocking factors. This approach ensures that all treatments have an equal chance of being applied to each unit, thereby mitigating systematic biases and facilitating straightforward inference.

A key tool for analyzing CRDs is the one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). The one-way ANOVA tests whether at least two of several treatment means differ significantly. Building upon principles of linear models, least squares estimation, and classical hypothesis testing, the one-way ANOVA provides a comprehensive framework to quantify uncertainty and make decisions about treatments based on observed data.

Experimental Designs and the Completely Randomized Design

Defining Experimental Design

An experimental design is a rule determining how experimental units are assigned to treatments. In a completely randomized design (CRD), no blocking factors are considered; all experimental units are assigned to treatments purely at random. This simplicity allows:

  • Equal probability of treatment assignment.
  • Avoidance of experimenter bias.
  • Straightforward statistical analysis.

Examples include testing different battery types or comparing rotary pump speeds to measure fluid flow. When no major nuisance factors (like day-to-day variation or spatial differences) need controlling, a CRD is often appropriate.

Randomization Procedure

Randomization ensures fairness and the validity of the statistical distributions underlying standard tests. Treatments, labeled 1 through \(v\), are assigned to experimental units by generating random numbers and sorting them. Modern software (R) or random number tables can be used. For instance, if testing three treatment levels (A, B, C) with equal sample sizes, we can:

  1. Generate random numbers for each unit.
  2. Rank units by their random numbers.
  3. Assign treatments in order based on the desired allocation.

This procedure ensures every arrangement is equally likely, aligning with the assumptions in ANOVA and related inference.

The One-Way ANOVA Model

Model Formulation

Consider \(v\) treatments with \(n\) observations each (for simplicity). Let \(Y_{ij}\) denote the response from the \(j\)-th unit receiving treatment \(i\), with \(i=1,\dots,v\) and \(j=1,\dots,n\). A common model is:

\[ Y_{ij} = \mu + \tau_i + \varepsilon_{ij}, \] {#eq:anova-model}

where:

  • \(\mu\) is the overall mean response.
  • \(\tau_i\) is the effect of the \(i\)-th treatment.
  • \(\varepsilon_{ij}\) are independent, normally distributed errors with mean 0 and variance \(\sigma^2\).

The assumptions are:

  • Errors \(\varepsilon_{ij}\) are i.i.d. \(N(0,\sigma^2)\).
  • Treatment effects sum to zero: \(\sum_{i=1}^v \tau_i = 0\) for identifiability.

This linear model model underpins the ANOVA. The null hypothesis for testing no treatment differences is \(H_0: \tau_1 = \tau_2 = \cdots = \tau_v = 0\).

Estimability and Least Squares Estimation

Not all model parameters (\(\tau_i\)) are independently estimable. However, estimable functions, linear combinations of parameters that correspond to expected values of observed data, can be estimated uniquely.

The least squares estimators (LSEs) solve the normal equations obtained by minimizing the sum of squared errors (SSE):

\[ \text{SSE} = \sum_{i=1}^v \sum_{j=1}^n (Y_{ij} - \hat{\mu}_i)^2, \] {#eq:sse}

where \(\hat{\mu}_i\) is the estimated mean for treatment \(i\). The LSE for each treatment mean \(\mu_i = \mu + \tau_i\) is simply the sample mean \(\bar{Y}_i = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n Y_{ij}\).

Because the normal equations are linearly dependent, an additional constraint is needed (e.g., \(\sum \tau_i = 0\)). Still, any estimable function (like differences between treatment means) can be uniquely estimated.

Distribution of Estimators

Under the normal error assumption, each estimator \(\bar{Y}_i\) is normally distributed, with:

\[ E(\bar{Y}_i) = \mu_i, \quad \text{Var}(\bar{Y}_i) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}. \] {#eq:estimator-variance}

The error variance \(\sigma^2\) is estimated by the mean square error (MSE):

\[ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{\text{SSE}}{N - v}, \] {#eq:mse}

where \(N = v \cdot n\) is the total number of observations.

Hypothesis Testing and ANOVA

The ANOVA F-Test

To test \(H_0: \tau_1 = \cdots = \tau_v = 0\) against \(H_a:\) at least two \(\tau_i\) differ, we use the ANOVA F-statistic:

\[ F = \frac{\text{MST}}{\text{MSE}}, \] {#eq:f-statistic}

where:

  • \(\text{SST}\) = treatment sum of squares measures variation among treatment means.
  • \(\text{MSE}\) = error mean square, \(\text{MSE} = \text{SSE}/(N-v)\).
  • \(\text{MST} = \text{SST}/(v-1)\).

Under \(H_0\), \(F \sim F_{v-1, N-v}\). If \(F\)-value exceeds a critical value (or the p-value is below a chosen \(\alpha\)), we reject \(H_0\).

Sample Size, Power, and Confidence Limits

Determining Sample Size and Power

To ensure the experiment can detect meaningful differences, consider the power analysis. Power calculations (detailed in referenced texts) help determine the number of replicates \(n\) per treatment to achieve a desired probability of detecting a specified difference at a given significance level \(\alpha\).

Use methods from Montgomery, Peck & Vining (2020) or Dean, Voss & Draguljić (2017), along with pilot data, to estimate \(\sigma^2\) and decide on sample size. If resources are limited, one may have to adjust objectives or accept lower power.

Confidence Limits for Variance

A one-sided confidence interval on \(\sigma^2\) prevents underestimating required observations. The upper confidence limit is derived from the chi-squared distribution:

\[ \frac{(N-v)\hat{\sigma}^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{N-v}. \] {#eq:variance-ci}

Such intervals guide planning by addressing uncertainty in the variance estimate.

Practical R Examples

Randomization in R

Suppose we have 3 treatments (A, B, C) and total 9 units (3 per treatment):

set.seed(123) # Total units
n <- 9 # Assign treatments to units, e.g., AAABBBCCC
treatments <- rep(c("A", "B", "C"), each = 3) # Assign random numbers
rand_nums <- runif(9) # Genrates random numbers
data_frame <- data.frame(Unit = 1:9, Treatment = treatments, RN = rand_nums)
data_frame_sorted <- data_frame[order(data_frame$RN), ] # Sort by RN
data_frame_sorted

This yields a random allocation order.

Explanation of the Key Functions in R

  • set.seed(123): Sets a seed for reproducibility.
  • rep(c("A", "B", "C"), each = 3): Creates a balanced treatment allocation, where each = 3 assigns 3 units to each treatment.
  • runif(9): Generates 9 random numbers from a uniform distribution.
  • order: Sorts the data frame based on the random numbers. The result of the function is row indices that would sort the data frame.

Fitting a One-Way ANOVA Model

Assume we have data from a soap experiment (soap.txt) with three soap types (regular, deodorant, moisturizing):

pacman::p_load(here)
# Read data
soap.data <- read.table(here("data","dean2017", "soap.txt"), header=TRUE)
soap.data$fSoap <- factor(soap.data$Soap)

# Fit a one-way ANOVA
fit <- aov(WtLoss ~ fSoap, data=soap.data)
summary(fit)

Estimating Means and Contrasts

We can estimate treatment means (least squares means) and test contrasts using emmeans or multcomp packages. For example:

Conclusion

A completely randomized design and its corresponding one-way ANOVA analysis form the bedrock of experimental inference when blocking is unnecessary. By carefully randomizing treatments, modeling responses, and applying least squares estimation, one can confidently infer whether treatments differ.

We introduced the conceptual framework and key formulae, showed how to conduct hypothesis tests and interpret ANOVA results, discussed sample size and power considerations, and provided R code for practical execution. Mastery of these fundamentals empowers researchers to design efficient experiments, maximize the utility of collected data, and confidently draw conclusions about treatment effects.

Appendix: Advanced Mathematical Details

A.1 Normal Equations and Estimability

The one-way ANOVA model can be written as:

\[ Y_{ij} = \mu_i + \varepsilon_{ij}, \quad \text{with } \mu_i = \mu + \tau_i. \]

The least squares approach forms normal equations from partial derivatives of SSE. Because \(\sum \tau_i = 0\), only differences in means are estimable. The space of estimable functions includes contrasts like \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\), but not individual \(\tau_i\) alone.

A.2 Distribution of the F-Statistic

Under \(H_0\):

\[ F = \frac{MST}{MSE} \sim F_{v-1, N-v}. \]

If treatments truly differ, F follows a noncentral F-distribution, enabling power calculations and minimum detectable differences.

A.3 Power Calculations

Power for detecting a difference \(\delta\) in means involves the noncentrality parameter of the F-distribution. By selecting \(\alpha, \sigma^2, \delta,\) and desired power, tables or software (e.g., pwr package in R) guide sample size choices.

# Example using pwr package
# install.packages("pwr") # if not installed
pacman::p_load(pwr)
pwr.anova.test(k = 3, f = 0.4, sig.level = 0.05, power = 0.8)
