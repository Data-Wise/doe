Introduction

Many real-world experiments involve multiple factors that may jointly influence a response. Factorial designs, especially two-factor designs, allow us to study each factor’s main effect and their interaction. Understanding interactions is crucial: sometimes one factor’s effect depends on the level of another, and ignoring these relationships can lead to misguided conclusions.

Intuition: Think of baking a cake (response): Factor A = Oven Temperature (low, medium, high), Factor B = Baking Time (short, medium, long). The sweetness or fluffiness of the cake (response) depends on both temperature and time. If at a high temperature, an extra-long baking time burns the cake, whereas at a low temperature, you might need longer time to get it fully baked. This interplay is the interaction effect—neither factor alone explains the outcome fully without considering the other.

Objectives

  1. Understand how two-factor factorial designs are structured and why they’re important.
  2. Distinguish between main effects and interaction effects in a two-way ANOVA.
  3. Conduct a two-way ANOVA, interpret results, and create interaction plots in R.
  4. Handle complexities such as unbalanced designs and understand different types of sums of squares.
  5. Explore both conceptual understanding (through analogies) and rigorous formulation (through mathematics and proofs in the Appendix).

Two-Factor Factorial Designs

Structure and Terminology

A two-factor design has two treatment factors, \(A\) and \(B\), with \(a\) and \(b\) levels each. Every combination of levels is tested, resulting in \(ab\) treatment combinations (sometimes called “cells”):

  • Factor \(A\): \(a\) levels \(A_1, A_2, \dots, A_a\)
  • Factor \(B\): \(b\) levels \(B_1, B_2, \dots, B_b\)

Example: In a crop yield experiment: - Factor A = Fertilizer type (3 levels) - Factor B = Irrigation method (2 levels) Total treatment combos = 3 × 2 = 6.

Each cell can have one or more observations (replicates).

Main Effects vs. Interaction

  • Main Effect (of A): The average change in response when we move across levels of A, ignoring B.
  • Main Effect (of B): The average change in response when we move across levels of B, ignoring A.

Interaction: Occurs if the effect of one factor on the response changes depending on the level of the other factor. If lines connecting treatment means in an interaction plot are not parallel, we have evidence of interaction.

Analogy: Think of wearing sunglasses (Factor A) and a hat (Factor B) on a sunny day. The effect of wearing sunglasses on how well you see (response) might differ depending on whether you’re also wearing a hat that shades your eyes. If sunglasses alone help a bit and the hat alone helps a bit, but together they help a lot more than expected by adding their individual effects, that’s an interaction.

Statistical Modeling: Two-Way ANOVA Models

The Full Model with Interaction

\[ Y_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij} + \epsilon_{ijk}, \]

where:

  • \(\mu\): Overall mean
  • \(\alpha_i\): Effect of the \(i\)-th level of Factor A (with \(\sum \alpha_i=0\))
  • \(\beta_j\): Effect of the \(j\)-th level of Factor B (with \(\sum \beta_j=0\))
  • \((\alpha\beta)_{ij}\): Interaction effect (with constraints \(\sum_i (\alpha\beta)_{ij}=0\) and \(\sum_j (\alpha\beta)_{ij}=0\))
  • \(\epsilon_{ijk} \sim N(0,\sigma^2)\): Random error

If replicates per treatment combination are \(n\), total observations \(N = abn\).

Reduced Models

  • No Interaction Model: If \((\alpha\beta)_{ij}=0\) for all \(i,j\), the model reduces to:

\[ Y_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + \epsilon_{ijk}. \]

  • Additivity: Without interaction, the effects of A and B simply add up.

Hypothesis Testing in Two-Way ANOVA

Hypotheses

  1. Interaction Test:
    • \(H_0:\) No interaction (\((\alpha\beta)_{ij}=0\))
    • \(H_a:\) Interaction present
  2. Main Effect of A:
    • \(H_0:\alpha_1=\alpha_2=\cdots=\alpha_a=0\)
    • \(H_a:\) At least one \(\alpha_i \neq 0\)
  3. Main Effect of B:
    • \(H_0:\beta_1=\beta_2=\cdots=\beta_b=0\)
    • \(H_a:\) At least one \(\beta_j \neq 0\)

Testing Order: Generally, check interaction first. If significant, main effects need careful interpretation.

ANOVA Table

Source DF SS MS F-test
A \(a-1\) \(SS_A\) \(MS_A\) \(F_A = MS_A/MS_E\)
B \(b-1\) \(SS_B\) \(MS_B\) \(F_B = MS_B/MS_E\)
A×B \((a-1)(b-1)\) \(SS_{AB}\) \(MS_{AB}\) \(F_{AB} = MS_{AB}/MS_E\)
Error \(N - ab\) \(SS_E\) \(MS_E\) -
Total \(N-1\) \(SS_T\) - -

Note: If data are unbalanced, different sum of squares types (Type I, II, III) might be considered. See Christensen (2018) for details.

Example (In R): Two-Way ANOVA with Interaction

Step-by-Step:

  1. Data Setup Suppose data has columns: Response, FactorA, and FactorB.

  2. Fit Model and ANOVA:

# Fit two-way ANOVA with interaction
model <- aov(Response ~ FactorA * FactorB, data = data)
summary(model)

Interpret the ANOVA table. Check if interaction is significant.

  1. Interaction Plot:
interaction.plot(data$FactorA, data$FactorB, data$Response)

Non-parallel lines indicate interaction.

  1. Interpretation: If interaction is significant:
    • The effect of one factor changes depending on the level of the other. If interaction is not significant:
    • Consider main effects separately.

Unbalanced Designs and Advanced Techniques

When sample sizes differ across cells, traditional ANOVA is complicated by the unequal representation of treatments. Approaches:

  • Type II SS: Marginal means ignoring higher-order terms.
  • Type III SS: Adjusted means considering all other factors.

R Implementation:

library(car)
Anova(model, type="II")  # Type II sums of squares
Anova(model, type="III") # Type III sums of squares

For deeper insights, see Dean et al. (2017) and Christensen (2018).

Visualizing and Understanding Interactions

Analogy: If you imagine factor A on the x-axis and the response on the y-axis, plotting a separate line for each level of factor B: - Parallel lines → No interaction (effects are additive). - Converging or crossing lines → Significant interaction (the effect of A depends on B).

Complex Example: Consider a pharmacological study: - Factor A = Drug Dosage (Low, Medium, High) - Factor B = Diet Type (Normal, High-Fat) Interaction plot might show that at Low dosage, both diets yield similar outcomes, but at High dosage, the High-Fat diet drastically reduces drug effectiveness, indicating a strong interaction.

Challenging Problems

  1. Unbalanced Two-Factor Design: Work with a dataset where one cell has fewer observations. Fit the model and compare Type I, II, and III sums of squares using car::Anova(model, type=…). Discuss how the choice of sum of squares affects conclusions about main and interaction effects.

  2. Complex Interaction Interpretation: Use a real or simulated dataset with a significant interaction. Implement emmeans in R to estimate marginal means and pairwise comparisons. Write a short report explaining how the interaction modifies the main effects. Include plots of estimated means and confidence intervals.

  3. Mathematical Proof of Interaction Contrasts (Appendix): Derive formulas for interaction contrasts and show how they measure the deviation from additivity. Prove that if all interaction contrasts are zero, the model is additive.

Conclusion

Two-factor factorial designs expand our analytical horizons, enabling the study of multi-dimensional treatment structures. Understanding and detecting interaction effects is critical—they can dramatically change conclusions about how factors influence a response. By mastering two-way ANOVA, sums of squares types, and interaction plots, graduate students gain a key skillset for robust, nuanced experimental analysis.

Appendix: Advanced Mathematical Derivations

Interaction Contrasts

An interaction can be represented by contrasts that compare how factor A’s differences vary across levels of factor B. For example, consider a simple 2×2 case (\(a=2,b=2\)):

\[ (\alpha\beta)_{11} - (\alpha\beta)_{12} - (\alpha\beta)_{21} + (\alpha\beta)_{22} \]

measures the extent to which effects deviate from additivity.

Additivity Condition

For no interaction:

\[ (\alpha\beta)_{ij} = 0 \implies \tau_{ij} = \alpha_i + \beta_j. \]

This forms a linear decomposition of cell means into separate contributions of factors A and B.

Proofs for Sums of Squares

Following Dean et al. (2017), sums of squares for main and interaction effects can be constructed from orthogonal contrasts. Derive the form of \(SS_A, SS_B, SS_{AB}\) from cell means \(\bar{Y}_{ij}\) and show their orthogonality in balanced designs.
